# A Tale of Two Archives: Cornelia Horsford and Julia Johnson

*Ellen Noonan*

As an introduction to the richness and gaps within Sylvester Manor’s archive, this extended essay (created by graduate students in New York University’s Fall 2024 Introduction to Public History course) presents a selection of archival items relating to two women central to the Manor’s history: Cornelia (Nellie) Conway Felton Horsford and Julia Dyd Havens Johnson. Cornelia, born on September 25, 1861, and who died on November 24, 1944, was the daughter of scientist Eben Norton Horsford and his second wife, Phoebe Dayton Gardiner. She resided primarily in Cambridge, Massachusetts, and spent her summers at Sylvester Manor. A published author with interests in archaeology and history, Cornelia inherited Sylvester Manor in 1901 and undertook a major renovation of the house and gardens in 1908. The Sylvester Manor archives are full of images and words by and about Cornelia. The archival traces of Julia’s life, though, are much more scant. She was a free woman of color, born in 1809 to Dido, an African American woman living on Shelter Island who had once been enslaved by a local family. Church records indicate that a second child born to Dido died that same year, making it likely that she had delivered twins and that Julia was the surviving child. No father has ever been listed for Julia, but documents indicate that she was bi-racial, the child of a white man. Julia worked for the Horsford family at Sylvester Manor and was regarded with great fondness by them; she died around 1907 or 1908.
