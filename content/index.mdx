## Reimagining the Sylvester Manor Archive

Sylvester Manor is known to most people as a place–a stately yellow home with acres of lawns, gardens, and woodlands nestled alongside Gardiner’s Creek on Shelter Island. But Sylvester Manor is also an archive, an extensive one assembled by generations of Sylvester family descendants, owners and residents of the property who both owned and employed countless numbers of people who labored there. In 2008, Sylvester descendants Alice Fiske and Eben Fiske Ostby donated [an archival collection of nearly five thousand items](https://findingaids.library.nyu.edu/fales/mss_208/) (including personal and business records, correspondence, and photographs) to the Special Collections division of New York University Libraries. 

This website is the work of students in New York University’s History Department, part of a multi-year collaboration with the [Sylvester Manor](http://sylvestermanor.org) historic site. Supported by a grant from the Mellon Foundation, History department students and faculty are digging into this extensive collection to showcase archival records that illuminate the collection’s potential for research and public projects focused on histories of marginalized and underrepresented people who lived and worked at Sylvester Manor, including enslaved people, indentured servants, Indigenous people, free people of color, working-class people, and women. 

Archival materials hold deep fascination, offering glimpses into the lives of people far removed from us in time. Items in an archive can be mundane or extraordinary, but all of them carry meanings that are historical, emotional, and, at bottom, political. Whose lives are documented in archives, how lives are documented in archives, and what meanings users draw from archives are all shaped by the forces of class, race, and gender that structure social status and power. The contents of an archive, no matter how numerous, can never tell a complete story about the past, but they do provide invaluable insights. In short, archives are what we make of them.
